§ 1

Executive Overview

3rd Eyes publishes meteorological signals for Kalshi daily high-temperature prediction markets. The core insight is that weather models, particularly the GFS ensemble mean and HRRR short-range radar assimilation, can identify temperature outcomes with sufficient confidence to generate positive expected value (EV) on Kalshi's binary NO contracts.

The strategy targets specific market inefficiencies: Kalshi market makers price temperature thresholds using basic climatological priors. When numerical weather prediction (NWP) models diverge strongly from those priors, due to an identifiable synoptic pattern, we publish a NO signal. The edge is meteorological, not statistical arbitrage.

Core Thesis in One Sentence

When two independent NWP models (GFS ensemble mean + HRRR hourly update) agree that a temperature threshold will NOT be reached, and the market is still pricing that event at 3–18¢ (implying 3–18% probability), the true probability is materially lower, creating positive EV on the NO side.

We operate exclusively on NO contracts, betting that a temperature threshold will not be exceeded (for high-temp markets) or not be reached (for low-temp markets). This creates asymmetric payouts: risk $X to win ~$Y where Y > X if probability is mispriced by ≥5 percentage points.

§ 2

Data Sources: GFS Ensemble + HRRR

🌐 GFS Ensemble Mean (GEFS)

Source: NOAA Global Ensemble Forecast System — 30-member ensemble

Resolution: 0.25° (~25 km) horizontal, 6-hourly runs (00Z, 06Z, 12Z, 18Z)

Lead time used: 12–96 hours (Day 0 through Day 4)

Role in signal: Primary temperature forecast. The ensemble mean suppresses random variability; the ensemble spread quantifies uncertainty. Narrow spread (low σ) = high model confidence.

Key metric: Ensemble mean 2m temperature vs. the market threshold. A calibrated minimum separation (the "buffer") is required before a signal is graded — exact thresholds are proprietary.

🛰 HRRR (High-Resolution Rapid Refresh)

Source: NOAA/ESRL HRRR — single deterministic model

Resolution: 3 km horizontal, hourly runs

Lead time used: 0–36 hours (same-day and next-day markets only)

Role in signal: Confirmation for near-term signals. HRRR assimilates real-time radar, surface observations, and satellite data — it has materially higher skill at ≤24 hours than GFS.

Key metric: HRRR maximum 2m temperature vs. Kalshi threshold. Agreement with GFS required for signal publication.

⚠️ Model Agreement Requirement: A signal is only published when both GFS ensemble mean and (for ≤36h markets) HRRR independently forecast the threshold will not be reached. Disagreement between models = no signal, regardless of individual model confidence.
Attribute GFS Ensemble (GEFS) HRRR
Best for Day 2–4 signals Same-day / Day 1 signals
Resolution ~25 km 3 km
Update frequency 4× daily Hourly
Ensemble size 30 members 1 (deterministic)
Radar assimilation No Yes (real-time)
Weakness Coarse resolution misses urban heat islands No ensemble spread — single point of failure

Signal Generation Pipeline

The signal pipeline runs automatically every 5 minutes, scanning open Kalshi weather markets and evaluating each against current NWP model output.

Step 1: Market Scan

Fetch all open Kalshi KXHIGH* and KXLOW* markets via the Kalshi API. Filter for markets resolving within the next 96 hours with ≥$10 liquidity on the NO side.

Step 2: NWP Temperature Pull

For each market, extract the forecast high temperature for the relevant city, date, and hour from GFS ensemble mean (all markets) and HRRR (markets resolving ≤36h).

Step 3: Buffer Calculation

Compute the temperature buffer: the gap between the forecast high and the Kalshi threshold:

buffer = | forecast high − market threshold |
A signal is published only when the buffer clears a calibrated,
grade-dependent minimum. Exact thresholds are proprietary.

A positive buffer means the model forecasts the threshold will NOT be reached. Larger buffer = higher confidence = higher signal grade.

Step 4: Dual-Model Confirmation

For ≤36h markets, HRRR must independently agree with the ensemble. For Day 2–4 markets, the GFS ensemble spread must fall within a calibrated tolerance; high spread = uncertain = no signal.

Step 5: Grade Assignment & Publish

Assign confidence grade (A / B / C), check orderbook depth for minimum fill ≥$8, and publish signal via Telegram with full reasoning: city, threshold, GFS mean, HRRR (if applicable), buffer, grade, and suggested position sizing.

Compounding Logic (Ladder Mode)

For subscribers using the compounding (Ladder) approach with 4 independent slots:

WIN: new_stake[slot] = payout_received ← compound gains
LOSS: new_stake[slot] = BASE_STAKE ← reset to base (no progressive loss)
SKIP: new_stake[slot] = unchanged ← thin fills don't reset compounding

Each slot compounds independently. A loss in Slot 1 does not affect Slots 2–4. This contains drawdown to individual slot level.

Stop-Loss Protocol

If an active position's NO price drops to ≤$0.82 (implying 18%+ probability the threshold will be reached), the signal system flags an exit recommendation. This recovers ~82¢ of every dollar at risk, limiting max loss to ~18% of notional per position.

Max loss per position ≈ stake × 0.18 + (contracts × $0.01 fee)
Example: $80 stake @ 95¢ NO → 84 contracts → max loss ≈ $80 × 0.18 + $0.84 = ~$15.24
Minimum Fill Threshold

Signals where the Kalshi orderbook cannot fill ≥$8 (thin markets, usually <10 contracts at the target NO price) are flagged as SKIP. These are published for informational purposes but are excluded from the W/L track record and do not affect slot compounding.

Kalshi Fee Structure

Kalshi charges a flat $0.01 per contract fee on fills. This fee applies to both entry and exit (stop-loss) fills.

Net profit per win = payout − entry_cost − (contracts × $0.01)
Example: 84 contracts @ 95¢ NO → win payout $84 − cost $79.80 − fee $0.84 = net +$3.36
Example: 84 contracts @ 97¢ NO → win payout $84 − cost $81.48 − fee $0.84 = net +$1.68

At high NO prices (95¢+), the fee is material (25–50% of gross profit). Always check net-of-fees EV before sizing up.

§ 6

Risk Management & Compounding

The 3rd Eyes framework prioritizes capital preservation over maximizing individual trade returns. This manifests in four hard rules:

Rule 1: Never size into thin orderbooks

Minimum fill $8. If the orderbook cannot fill $8 at the target price, skip the trade entirely. Thin markets mean: poor price execution, high slippage on exit, and outsized fee impact.

Rule 2: Respect the stop-loss unconditionally

Stop-loss at 82¢ is not a suggestion. When the NO price drops to ≤82¢, the position is exiting a scenario where our model edge has deteriorated (unexpected weather development). Holding through a stop risks full position loss.

Rule 3: No pyramiding into losing positions

If a position moves against us, we do not add contracts. The stop-loss exits the position. We wait for the next independent signal.

Rule 4: Slot isolation

Each of the 4 slots is financially independent. Slot 1 losing does not trigger additional exposure from Slots 2–4. Maximum simultaneous exposure = 4 × slot_stake, and each slot resets independently on loss.
